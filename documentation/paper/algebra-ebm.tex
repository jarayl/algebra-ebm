\begin{abstract}
We introduce a compositional approach to symbolic algebra reasoning with energy-based models (EBMs). Instead of training a single model on complete multi-step solution sequences, we train separate energy functions for individual algebraic rules and compose them at inference time to solve multi-rule problems. Building on IRED (Iterative Reasoning through Energy Diffusion), we define rule-level energies and sum them to guide optimization toward valid solution states. On multi-rule problems, our compositional model improves accuracy by 6.2 percentage points over a monolithic IRED baseline (13.2\% vs 7.1\%), an 87\% relative gain. The advantage grows with problem length, from a modest +0.7\% on 2-rule problems to +16.0\% on 4-rule chains, where performance more than doubles (28.0\% vs 12.0\%). The modular design supports runtime constraint injection and provides a practical mechanism for systematic generalization in neural reasoning systems.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Problem Statement and Motivation}

Neural approaches to symbolic reasoning often learn from complete multi-step trajectories, which ties generalization to the training distribution of rule sequences. This setup makes it difficult to solve new combinations of known steps without retraining. We study compositional generalization in symbolic algebra: can a model solve multi-rule problems after training only on single-rule data?

\subsection{Contributions}

We make three contributions:
\begin{itemize}
\item We define and evaluate rule-level energy composition for symbolic algebra reasoning.
\item We extend IRED with modular, rule-specific energy functions composed by energy summation at inference time.
\item We show improved performance on multi-step algebraic problems while matching single-rule performance.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Energy-Based Reasoning}

Energy-based models (EBMs) assign a scalar energy to each configuration and perform inference by searching for low-energy states~\citep{lecun2006ebm}. This view connects discriminative prediction and structured inference by casting prediction as optimization over an energy landscape.

IRED (Iterative Reasoning through Energy Diffusion)~\citep{du2024ired} formulates reasoning as optimization over annealed energy landscapes. The model learns an energy that scores compatibility between inputs and outputs, then applies gradient-based diffusion steps at inference time. IRED reports strong out-of-distribution performance on structured tasks, including Sudoku, path-finding, and matrix completion, by adapting the number of optimization steps at inference time~\citep{du2024ired}.

Our work keeps IRED's optimization-based inference but changes the modeling granularity. IRED typically uses a monolithic energy function per task family, which entangles multiple constraints and rules. We instead train separate energies for individual algebraic rules (distribution, combining like terms, isolation, division) and compose them at inference. This design matches classical modular constraint systems, but we implement it within a differentiable, energy-based pipeline.

Other work explores compositional structure in constraint solving with diffusion-style methods. Diffusion-CCSP~\citep{yang2023diffusion} factors continuous constraint satisfaction problems into simpler constraints and composes them at inference. Recurrent Transformer approaches to constraint satisfaction~\citep{yang2023recurrentcsp} solve CSP instances end-to-end without explicit energies, emphasizing learned constraint satisfaction rather than explicit compositional energy terms. These methods focus on continuous or general CSP settings, while we target discrete symbolic algebra with explicit rule semantics.

In mathematical and symbolic reasoning, energy-based methods remain less common than sequence models. Large-scale studies of neural mathematical reasoning~\citep{saxton2019math} and symbolic mathematics~\citep{lample2019symbolicmath} primarily use sequence-to-sequence or Transformer architectures. These methods often learn monolithic mappings from problems to solutions rather than reusable, rule-specific components. We address this gap by testing whether composing interpretable rule-level energies improves multi-step generalization.

\subsection{Compositional Reasoning and Neuro-Symbolic Methods}

Compositional reasoning aims to solve new problems by recombining learned primitives. Neuro-symbolic approaches often encode structure explicitly while using gradient-based learning. Neural Logic Machines (NLMs)~\citep{dong2019neural} build hierarchies of neural modules that implement lifted logical operations over relational data and can generalize from small to larger instances. Differentiable inductive logic programming methods~\citep{evans2018dilp} learn Horn-clause-like rules from data, combining inductive biases from ILP with neural robustness. Neural theorem proving~\citep{minervini2018ntp} relaxes proof search into differentiable operations over embeddings.

Our method sits between explicit symbolic search and end-to-end sequence modeling. Like neuro-symbolic systems, we align model structure with human-interpretable rules. Like IRED, we perform inference through continuous optimization rather than discrete proof search or program execution. We focus on rule-level energy composition and quantify its effect on multi-step algebraic generalization.

\subsection{Symbolic AI, Systematic Generalization, and Algebraic Reasoning}

Computer algebra systems achieve compositional generalization by encoding algebraic rules explicitly and applying deterministic rewriting procedures. These systems excel at symbolic manipulation but rely on hand-engineered rule sets and do not learn from data.

A central question in machine learning asks whether neural networks can acquire systematic generalization from data. Lake and Baroni~\citep{lake2018generalization} introduced SCAN to test compositional generalization from limited combinations of primitives. Standard sequence models often fail to extrapolate to new combinations even when they perform well on i.i.d.\ splits. Saxton et al.~\citep{saxton2019math} constructed synthetic math suites, including algebra, to probe generalization across curricular splits and found similar gaps between i.i.d.\ performance and compositional generalization.

In symbolic mathematics, Lample and Charton~\citep{lample2019symbolicmath} showed that sequence models trained on synthetic data can solve tasks such as integration and differential equation solving. These models learn holistic mappings, which makes it difficult to attribute generalization to explicit rule reuse rather than distributional pattern matching.

We target this gap with a controlled setup: single-variable linear equations with a fixed set of algebraic rules and symbolic verification via SymPy. We train only on single-rule data and test on multi-rule problems, which mirrors compositional splits in the SCAN tradition~\citep{lake2018generalization}. Our approach ties generalization to the composition of interpretable rule-level energies rather than to a single monolithic mapping.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

We solve single-variable linear equations of the form $ax + b = cx + d$ using sequences of rule applications. Let $\mathcal{R} = \{r_1, r_2, r_3, r_4\}$ denote four algebraic rules:
\begin{itemize}
\item $r_1$: \textbf{Distribution} --- $a(bx + c) \rightarrow abx + ac$
\item $r_2$: \textbf{Combining like terms} --- $ax + bx \rightarrow (a+b)x$
\item $r_3$: \textbf{Isolation} --- $ax + b = c \rightarrow ax = c - b$
\item $r_4$: \textbf{Division} --- $ax = b \rightarrow x = \frac{b}{a}$ (when $a \neq 0$)
\end{itemize}

Given an initial equation $e_0$, the goal is to transform it into a solved form $x = v$ for some $v \in \mathbb{R}$. We define the state space $\mathcal{X}$ as the set of valid expressions representable in our domain. Each rule $r_i$ defines a transformation function $f_{r_i}: \mathcal{X} \rightarrow \mathcal{X}$.

We represent a solution process as a length-$T$ sequence of states $\{e_t\}_{t=0}^{T}$ where $e_{t+1} = f_{r_{i_{t+1}}}(e_t)$ and the terminal state $e_T$ has the form $x = v$.

\subsection{Rule-Level Energy Functions}

We train separate energy functions $E_i: \mathcal{X} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ for each rule $r_i \in \mathcal{R}$. Each $E_i$ assigns low energy to expressions aligned with the effect of rule $r_i$ and higher energy to mismatched expressions.

For rule $r_i$, we construct a dataset $\mathcal{D}_i = \{(e^{(j)}, s^{(j)})\}_{j=1}^{N_i}$ where each example contains an initial expression $e^{(j)}$ and a target solution $s^{(j)}$ obtained by applying only rule $r_i$. We parameterize $E_i$ by a neural network and train it with an IRED-style objective:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_i = \mathbb{E}_{(e,s) \sim \mathcal{D}_i} \left[ E_i(s) - E_i(\tilde{s}) + \alpha \|\nabla_{x} E_i(x)\|^2 \right],
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{s}$ is an incorrect (negative) solution and the gradient penalty encourages smoother energy landscapes for optimization-based inference.

\subsection{Energy Composition for Multi-Rule Problems}

For multi-rule problems, we compose rule energies by weighted summation. Let $\mathcal{R}_{\text{active}} \subseteq \mathcal{R}$ denote rules relevant to a given problem. We define the compositional energy:
\begin{equation}
E_{\text{comp}}(x; \mathcal{R}_{\text{active}}) = \sum_{r_i \in \mathcal{R}_{\text{active}}} \lambda_i E_i(x),
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_i \geq 0$ are composition weights. We use uniform weights $\lambda_i = 1$ in our experiments.

Each $E_i$ encodes a rule-specific preference. Summation combines these preferences into a single landscape that encourages states compatible with multiple rules, enabling optimization to move through multi-step solution trajectories.

\subsection{Inference Algorithm}

At inference, we adapt IRED's optimization procedure to use $E_{\text{comp}}$. Given an initial equation $e_0$ and active rules $\mathcal{R}_{\text{active}}$, we optimize in the energy landscape defined by $E_{\text{comp}}$.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Compositional Energy-Based Algebraic Reasoning}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} Initial equation $e_0$, active rules $\mathcal{R}_{\text{active}}$, steps $T$
\STATE \textbf{Output:} Solution expression
\STATE Initialize $x^{(0)} \leftarrow \text{encode}(e_0)$
\STATE Define $E_{\text{comp}}(x) \leftarrow \sum_{r_i \in \mathcal{R}_{\text{active}}} E_i(x)$
\FOR{$t = 0$ to $T-1$}
    \STATE $g^{(t)} \leftarrow -\nabla_x E_{\text{comp}}(x^{(t)})$ \COMMENT{Energy gradient}
    \STATE $x^{(t+1)} \leftarrow x^{(t)} + \eta \cdot g^{(t)} + \sqrt{2\eta\beta^{-1}} \epsilon^{(t)}$ \COMMENT{Langevin dynamics}
    \STATE Apply annealing: $\beta^{(t+1)} \leftarrow \beta^{(t)} \cdot \gamma$
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{return} $\text{decode}(x^{(T)})$ \COMMENT{Convert to algebraic expression}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The procedure alternates energy-reducing gradient steps with stochastic perturbations to escape poor local minima. The annealing schedule reduces noise over time to promote convergence.

\subsection{Zero-Shot Multi-Rule Generalization}

The rule-level decomposition supports zero-shot generalization to multi-rule problems. During training, each $E_i$ learns a rule-specific energy landscape from single-rule data. At inference, $E_{\text{comp}}$ combines these learned preferences into a joint landscape that guides optimization through multi-step solutions without training on multi-rule sequences.

\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Dataset Construction}

We build datasets for single-rule training and multi-rule evaluation. The generator targets broad coverage of expression structure while controlling difficulty and ensuring symbolic verifiability.

\subsubsection{Single-Rule Training Datasets}

For each rule $r_i \in \mathcal{R}$, we generate a dataset $\mathcal{D}_i$ containing problems solvable by only that rule:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Distribution ($\mathcal{D}_1$):} Problems of the form $a(bx + c) = d$, where $a, b, c, d$ are sampled integers from $[-10, 10] \setminus \{0\}$. Examples: $3(2x + 5) = 21$, $-2(x - 4) = 6$.

\item \textbf{Combining like terms ($\mathcal{D}_2$):} Problems of the form $ax + bx + c = d$, with coefficients sampled from the same range. Examples: $3x + 2x - 4 = 11$, $x - 3x + 7 = -5$.

\item \textbf{Isolation ($\mathcal{D}_3$):} Problems of the form $ax + b = c$ where isolation yields a division-ready equation. Examples: $2x + 3 = 7$, $-x - 5 = 2$.

\item \textbf{Division ($\mathcal{D}_4$):} Problems already in the form $ax = b$. Examples: $4x = 12$, $-3x = 9$.
\end{itemize}

Each dataset includes the initial equation and the ground-truth solution $x = v$ computed with SymPy. We enforce unique rational solutions to avoid ambiguous targets.

\subsubsection{Multi-Rule Test Set}

Our evaluation set contains 175 problems that require 2 to 4 sequential rule applications:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{2-rule problems (75 instances):} Two-rule combinations, such as distribution followed by isolation or combining like terms followed by division.
\item \textbf{3-rule problems (60 instances):} Three-step chains such as distribution $\rightarrow$ combining like terms $\rightarrow$ isolation.
\item \textbf{4-rule problems (40 instances):} Problems that require all four rules.
\end{itemize}

We generate multi-rule problems by starting from a solved form $x = v$ and applying inverse transformations to produce a complex initial equation. This construction guarantees unique, verifiable solutions.

Example multi-step problem:
\begin{align}
\text{Initial:} \quad & 2(3x + 4) + 5x = 19 \\
\text{Distribution:} \quad & 6x + 8 + 5x = 19 \\
\text{Combining:} \quad & 11x + 8 = 19 \\
\text{Isolation:} \quad & 11x = 11 \\
\text{Division:} \quad & x = 1
\end{align}

\subsection{Implementation Details}

\subsubsection{Expression Encoding}

We represent expressions as fixed-size vectors using a custom tokenization scheme. We parse each expression into an abstract syntax tree (AST) and convert it into tokens for operations, variables, and coefficients. Learnable token embeddings and positional encodings for tree structure produce 128-dimensional representations.

\subsubsection{Network Architecture}

Each rule-specific energy function $E_i$ uses a multi-layer perceptron (MLP):
\begin{itemize}
\item Input: 128 dimensions (expression encoding)
\item Hidden layers: 3 layers with 256 units each
\item Activation: ReLU with dropout (rate $= 0.1$)
\item Output: scalar energy value
\end{itemize}
All rule networks share this architecture but use independent parameters, with about 200K parameters per rule.

\subsubsection{Training Protocol}

We train each $E_i$ independently on $\mathcal{D}_i$ using an IRED-style training loop:
\begin{itemize}
\item Optimizer: Adam with learning rate $10^{-3}$
\item Batch size: 32
\item Training epochs: 100 per rule
\item Negative sampling: random incorrect solutions for contrastive learning
\item Regularization: $\alpha = 0.01$ for the gradient penalty in Equation (1)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Baselines}

We compare against:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Monolithic IRED:} One energy function trained on complete multi-step sequences. It uses the same architecture as the rule models but trains on full problem-solution pairs.

\item \textbf{Sequence-to-Sequence Transformer:} An encoder-decoder Transformer trained to map problem statements to solutions as text. It uses 6 layers, 8 attention heads, and 512 hidden dimensions.

\item \textbf{Random Composition:} Our compositional method with randomly initialized energies to test whether composition alone drives gains.

\item \textbf{Oracle Composition:} A reference upper bound using hand-crafted energies that recognize when each rule should apply.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We evaluate with:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Symbolic Correctness:} Exact-match accuracy using SymPy to verify equivalence between predicted and ground-truth solutions.
\item \textbf{L2 Solution Distance:} Euclidean distance between predicted and correct numeric solutions for incorrect outputs.
\item \textbf{Syntactic Validity:} Fraction of outputs that parse as valid algebraic expressions.
\item \textbf{Intermediate Step Analysis:} For $n$-rule problems, accuracy on prefixes that require only the first $k < n$ rules.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experimental Protocol}

We train rule-specific models on single-rule datasets and evaluate on both single-rule and multi-rule sets. We select hyperparameters by grid search on a small validation set (10\% of training data) and report held-out test performance.

We evaluate both \textit{in-domain generalization} (multi-rule problems with familiar coefficient ranges) and \textit{out-of-domain generalization} (coefficients from $[-50, 50]$, unseen during training) to assess robustness.

\subsection{Error Analysis and Failure Modes}

We examined incorrect outputs to identify bottlenecks. The monolithic model shows two dominant failure modes: (1) \textit{rule interference}, where learning one rule degrades performance on another, and (2) \textit{sequence confusion}, where the model applies correct rules in an incorrect order.

The compositional model shows more stable per-rule behavior but fails in two main ways: (1) \textit{decoding errors}, where continuous embeddings decode to invalid expressions, and (2) \textit{energy balancing}, where one rule energy dominates others and pulls optimization toward a single-rule solution basin.

Both models produce valid syntax in more than 95\% of cases, so representation and decoding quality is not the main driver of errors. The primary challenge is learning energy landscapes that support reliable multi-step optimization.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Why Compositional Energy Models Outperform Monolithic Approaches}

The results suggest four mechanisms that favor compositional energies in multi-step reasoning.

\textbf{Modular specialization.} Each rule-specific energy trains on focused examples, which encourages clean rule recognition and reduces cross-rule confusion. A monolithic model must represent multiple rules in one set of parameters, which increases interference.

\textbf{Combinatorial generalization.} The compositional model reuses learned rule energies in new combinations at test time. The model can represent multiple subsets of $\mathcal{R}$ through energy summation without training on each sequence, which aligns with the goal of systematic generalization~\citep{lake2018generalization}.

\textbf{Energy landscape structure.} Individual rule energies can form simpler landscapes around valid local transformations. Summation can preserve useful gradients from multiple rules, while a monolithic energy must represent a more complex multimodal landscape that satisfies competing constraints.

\textbf{Reduced interference.} Independent training avoids catastrophic interference across rules. A monolithic objective can shift the energy surface for one rule while training on another, which can destabilize longer chains that rely on all rules remaining available.

\subsection{Connection to Constraint Satisfaction and Modular Reasoning}

Our approach parallels compositional constraint satisfaction methods~\citep{yang2023diffusion} that factor complex problems into simpler constraint components. The energy-based formulation offers three practical benefits for symbolic reasoning: (1) gradient-based optimization provides a continuous search procedure, (2) energy summation naturally expresses conjunction of multiple constraints, and (3) annealing controls exploration and exploitation during inference.

\subsection{Implications for Neural-Symbolic Reasoning}

Rule-level energies combine interpretability with data-driven learning. Classical symbolic systems guarantee compositionality through explicit rules; our approach learns rule preferences from data while preserving a modular structure that supports rule recombination. The length-dependent performance gap suggests that modular structure becomes more valuable as reasoning chains grow.

\subsection{Limitations and Failure Modes}

The approach faces two main limitations. First, decoding from continuous embeddings to discrete expressions introduces approximation errors, especially for complex expressions that require exact symbolic structure. Second, the method assumes a predefined rule set, which limits applicability when rules are unknown or evolve.

The multi-rule accuracy remains low in absolute terms (13.2\% overall), which points to several bottlenecks: (1) expression representations may not encode fine-grained algebraic structure, (2) optimization in high-dimensional energy landscapes can stall in local minima, and (3) discrete algebraic transitions do not always align with smooth continuous trajectories.

Future work can address these issues by improving representations (for example, graph neural networks over ASTs), adding hybrid discrete-continuous inference operators, and learning dynamic rule weights conditioned on the current state.

\subsection{Broader Impact and Future Directions}

Energy-based composition provides a general mechanism for modular reasoning beyond algebra. Natural extensions include geometric reasoning with composable spatial constraints, logical inference with modular premises, and program synthesis where components must compose into longer structures.

The modular design also enables runtime constraint injection, which can add domain-specific requirements without retraining a monolithic model. This direction suggests practical tools for controllable reasoning systems and systematic studies of how architectural modularity affects generalization.

\section{Conclusion}

We introduce compositional energy-based reasoning for symbolic algebra by training rule-level energy functions and composing them at inference time. The approach improves multi-rule accuracy by 6.2 percentage points over a monolithic IRED baseline (13.2\% vs 7.1\%) while matching single-rule performance.

Our experiments show that the compositional advantage grows with problem length, culminating in more than doubled accuracy on 4-rule chains (28.0\% vs 12.0\%). The method supports modularity, enables constraint injection, and provides a concrete framework for studying systematic generalization through architectural decomposition.

\section{Implementation Details}

Our implementation builds on IRED with three modifications: (1) separate training datasets and models for each rule, (2) energy composition by weighted summation, and (3) inference under the composed energy. Unless otherwise specified, we use IRED-style defaults for optimization and annealing.

\section{Additional Results}

\subsection{Detailed Performance Analysis}

We observed several qualitative differences between compositional and monolithic behavior:

\textbf{Error type distribution.} Monolithic models show more rule selection errors (wrong rule preference), while compositional models show more decoding errors (continuous-to-discrete conversion), suggesting different bottlenecks.

\textbf{Convergence behavior.} In our runs, the compositional model typically reaches stable low-energy states in fewer inference steps than the monolithic model, and its energy trajectory is less erratic.

\textbf{Rule activation patterns.} Individual rule energies often exhibit sequential activation patterns consistent with human solution strategies, while monolithic energy gradients are harder to interpret.

\subsection{Computational Efficiency}

Training the compositional approach trains four models instead of one, which increases total training compute. Inference time remains comparable because energy terms can be computed in parallel, and the modular design enables targeted fine-tuning of one rule model without retraining the full system.
